## Banana (*Musa* spp., predominantly Cavendish AAA)

Banana is a continuous-cropping monocot herb. The mother plant produces one bunch and dies; ratoons (suckers) follow. Cycle from planting to first harvest is **11-16 months**, depending on cultivar and elevation. South African production is concentrated in KZN North Coast, Mpumalanga Lowveld, and Limpopo (Tzaneen / Letsitele). Cavendish dominates >95% of commercial plantings.

| Factor | Multiplier | Source |
|---|---|---|
| Ratoon vs plant crop | 0.85-0.90 × N/K (ratoons remobilise N, K, S, B, Mn, Cu from previous mother) | [T2 Yara banana §Application strategies] |
| Trash retained on field | 0.85 × K (recycled) | [T2 IPNI K recycling] |

| High-density planting (>2,000/ha) | 1.10-1.20 × all macros | [T2 Haifa Banana §Plant density] |

| Drought / dry winter | reduce N until rain returns | [T2 Haifa Banana §Application timing] |

### Notes
- **K is the critical macro.** N:K ratio in fertigation should sit between **1:1 and 1:1.6**. Excess K restricts Ca uptake → "soft finger" disorder and reduced fruit-stem strength.
- **Salt-sensitive.** Avoid KCl on light-textured soils; Cl >0.4% in leaf is toxic.
- **Continuous fertigation preferred.** Annual rates split into 24-52 weekly doses give the best K-use efficiency. Granular-only programmes lose ~25-30% of K via leaching on coarse sands.

## Pineapple (*Ananas comosus*, Smooth Cayenne / MD-2 / Queen)

Pineapple is a CAM monocot bromeliad. The cycle from planting (sucker, slip, or crown) to first fruit harvest is **18-24 months** (12-18 vegetative + 5-6 fruit development). SA production is concentrated in Eastern Cape (East London / Bathurst — Queen) and KZN North Coast (Cayenne / hybrid). Floral induction is **artificially triggered with ethephon or ethylene gas** once plants reach target D-leaf weight (~80 g for Cayenne).

### Adjustments

| Factor | Multiplier | Source |
|---|---|---|

### Notes
- **Floral induction is the most important control point.** N must drop to near-zero 4-8 weeks before forcing — residual N delays floral transition or produces multiple crowns. K should be loaded heavy in the same window so the plant has reserves for fruit fill.
- **CAM physiology means slow nutrient uptake.** Frequent low doses outperform occasional heavy applications.
- **Acid-loving.** Avoid liming above pH 5.5; high pH locks out Fe and Mn fast.
- **SA Eastern Cape Queen** is a dryland production with much lower yields and a longer cycle (24+ mo). KZN Cayenne under irrigation hits the upper benchmark.

## Papaya (*Carica papaya*, predominantly Solo / Sunrise / Hortus Gold in SA)

Papaya is a short-cycle perennial dicot, **continuously fruiting** from ~6-8 mo post-transplant onwards. Commercially treated as 18-24 mo (replant at year 3 due to declining yield + virus pressure). SA production: KZN North Coast (small commercial), Mpumalanga / Limpopo (mostly informal). Vulnerable to **PRSV** (papaya ringspot virus) — drives short orchard life.

### Notes
- **Boron is the headline micro.** Bumpy / lumpy fruit deformity is almost always B-driven on SA sandy soils. Maintain leaf B 20-40 ppm; pre-flowering Solubor non-negotiable.
- **N:K ratio of 1:1 to 1:1.5** in fertigation maintains fruit quality. Excess N delays flowering and pushes vegetative growth at expense of fruit set.
- **Continuous fruiting means continuous demand.** Programme as 12 monthly fertigations rather than 4 quarterly applications.
- **PRSV is the binding constraint** on SA papaya orchard life — the agronomy programme should plan a 18-30 mo cycle, not "perennial" in the long-life sense.

## Olive (*Olea europaea*; SA cultivars: Mission, Frantoio, Manzanilla, Kalamata, Barouni, Coratina)

Mediterranean evergreen perennial. SA production is concentrated in Western Cape (Paarl, Stellenbosch, Robertson, Riebeek-Kasteel) and Klein Karoo. Pronounced **alternate bearing** (biennial yield oscillation). Production is a deliberate trade-off between **fruit yield, oil content, and oil quality** (polyphenols, peroxide, free fatty acids).

### Notes
- **Calcium is the headline macro** for olives, per the SA industry — soil Ca threshold is the first thing to fix on acid Western Cape sands.
- **Boron is the headline micro.** Pre-bloom Solubor is a near-universal SA practice. The 14-185 ppm leaf band is wide; aim for 30-50 ppm.
- **Alternate bearing cannot be fertilised away.** Crop thinning during "on" years is the structural fix; nutrient programme can damp amplitude but not eliminate it.
- **Oil quality vs yield trade-off.** Above 1.7-2.0% leaf N, oil free fatty acids and peroxide values rise. Push N hard for table-olive yield; pull back for oil-quality cultivars (Frantoio, Coratina).
- **SA-specific cease point.** Stop N applications by **end of autumn (May)** to avoid winter flush + frost damage.
- **Chl toxicity threshold:** leaf Cl >0.5% is symptomatic. Avoid KCl on coastal / saline sites; substitute K₂SO₄ or KNO₃.
